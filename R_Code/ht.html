Hypothesis Tests

For all of variables of interest, we compute substantively meaningful quantities of interest and their confidence intervals rather than relying directly on a table of model coefficients. The quantities of interest (QIs) are as follows:

  • First-Difference (FD). This is just the probability of answering correctly when the variable of interest takes on a high value minus the probability of answering correctly with the variable of interest takes on a low value. In the case of education, these values are 6 (a college degree) and 3 (a high school diploma), respectively. This does not account for ceiling and floor effects.
  • Odds-Ratio (OR). This QI makes an explicit attempts to control for ceiling and floor effects, because odds-ratios are constant regardless of the other variables and their coefficients. This is equivalent to simply inspecting the coefficients, although the change we consider is more substantively interesting.
  • Risk-Ratio (RR). This QI tells us how much more likely those with high values of the explanatory variable are to answer correctly than those that have low values of the explanatory variable. It treats a change from 0.01 to 0.02 and 0.25 to 0.50 equals effect sizes. In in both cases, the risk-ratio is 2, meaning that the change in the explanatory variable doubles the chance of answering correctly. Note that this partial addresses concerns about floor effects, but worsens any concerns about ceiling effects. Note that a risk-ration of 1.5 means that the chance of answering correctly increases by 50% when the explanatory variable is high rather than low.
